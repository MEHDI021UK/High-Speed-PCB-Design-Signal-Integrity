## 1. Introduction to High-Speed PCB Design

### 1.1 PCB Design Flow (General)

- Product concept → requirements/specs → schematic (with high-speed and power nets marked) → PCB design plan → stackup and technology choice → layout (footprints, DRC, controlled impedance, placement, routing) → DFM/DFA review → fabrication and assembly.
- In schematic: group blocks (e.g. Ethernet on one page), mark differential/single-ended impedance, high current and voltage; validate BOM.
- In layout: assess high-speed nature and complexity first; then choose stackup and routing strategy.

### 1.2 How to Decide If It Is High-Speed

**Step 1 – Get one of:** maximum frequency content **Fm**, fastest rise/fall time **Tr**, or max data transfer rate **DTR**.

Relation: **Fm ≈ 0.5 / Tr ≈ 2.5 × DTR**

Examples:

- Tr = 100 ps → Fm ≈ 5 GHz  
- DTR = 5 Gbps → Fm ≈ 12.5 GHz  

**Rule of thumb:** If **Fm > 50 MHz**, treat as high-speed (with exceptions: sometimes 60 MHz can be relaxed, sometimes 40 MHz needs care).

**Step 2 – Wavelength and length rule**

- **λm = v / Fm** (v = signal speed on PCB, see §3).
- If trace length **l < λm/12** → may treat as low-speed; if **l ≥ λm/12** → treat as high-speed.
- If the path leaves the board (cable + connector), use **total** interconnection length.

**Examples (FR4, v ≈ 5.9 in/ns stripline / 6.9 microstrip):**

- Fm = 50 MHz → λm/12 ≈ 9.8 in → small boards often low-speed; longer boards high-speed.
- Fm = 100 MHz → λm/12 ≈ 4.9 in.
- Fm ≥ 1 GHz → λm/12 ≤ 0.5 in → almost always high-speed.

Board size and trace length matter as much as frequency.

### 1.3 Design Complexity Factors

- Fine-pitch BGAs (0.5 mm, 0.4 mm), pin count, interfaces (HDMI, GbE, DDR, etc.).
- Small board with many components; large board with 500+ components.
- Impedance control (e.g. 100 Ω diff, 50 Ω SE) with many components.
- Layout time > two weeks; high pin density (>110 pins/in²); high part density (>10 parts/in²).
- Mixed digital/RF/analog; mixed materials; tight electrical constraints; 8–12+ layers.

### 1.4 High-Speed Design Considerations (Six Areas)

1. **Transmission lines** – High-speed nets as controlled-impedance transmission lines to limit distortion, crosstalk, and radiation.
2. **Signal degradation** – Keep reflections, crosstalk, attenuation, ground bounce, etc., within acceptable limits.
3. **Technology** – Stackup and process (e.g. HDI, blind/buried vias) must support density and protocols.
4. **EMI** – Control emissions and susceptibility.
5. **Power integrity** – Stable supply at devices despite high-frequency noise on power/ground.
6. **Routing** – Topology, length matching, return path, vias, and plane usage.

These map to: **Signal Integrity + Stackup** (1–3), **EMI** (4), **Power Integrity** (5), **Layout** (6).

**Phase 1 – Ideation & Feasibility (2–6 weeks)**  
Quick feasibility: component selection, rough BoM cost, power budget, risk analysis.

**Phase 2 – Concept & Specification (2–6 weeks)**  
Turn product needs into **electronics specs** (interfaces, power, environment, compliance).

**Phase 3 – Schematic & PCB Design (1+ month)**  
- Full schematic capture; review by firmware or another engineer.  
- Component library; PCB layout (2–6 layers).  
- **Design for Manufacture & Test:** DFM, DFA, DFT; thermal & EMI considered up front.

**DFM (Design for Manufacturability)**  
- Minimum track/spacing ≥ 0.15 mm (avoid 0.1 mm).  
- All vias ≥ 0.25 mm finished hole (no microvias unless needed → cheaper).  
- 0.2 mm solder-mask expansion; silkscreen never on pads.  
- Panelisation in 300 × 250 mm frames (V-score or tab-routing).  
- Fiducials on all four corners of every board in panel.  
- No components within 3 mm of board edge or V-score.

**DFA (Design for Assembly)**  
- All components on one side if possible.  
- Connector and part polarisation clear.  
- Thermal pads on GND with via-in-pad + resin fill (flat board during reflow).

**DFT (Design for Testability)**  
- Dedicated test points; LED “heartbeat.”  
- Test points for power rails and critical nets/sections.

**DFC (Design for Cost)**  
- Prefer 0402 over 0603 where applicable.  
- Dual-source every part (e.g. DigiKey + alternate distributor).

**DFS (Design for Supply and Security)**  
- All parts from at least two manufacturers or distributors.  
- Critical ICs (MCU, radio): 24‑month lifecycle guarantee.  
- Secure element or TrustZone-ready MCU (e.g. for GDPR).  
- Unique device secret keys injected in production (e.g. encrypted JTAG).  
- PCB tamper-detect mesh (e.g. two-layer serpentine on outer layers).

**DFE (Design for EMC)**  
*Check device class: 1 (one-time use), 2 (daily use), or 3 (human/medical/defence).*  
- Target **EN 301 489** + **EN 55032 Class B** first time.  
- 4-layer board with solid GND on layer 2; high-speed (SPI, I2C) on inner layers.  
- 0402 ferrite on VCC entry; 100 nF + 10 µF decoupling per IC.  
- Stitching vias every ~5 mm around board edge; metal shield can over radio with grounding fingers.  
- Y-caps on mains side if touching 230 V.  
- *Example:* Switch in EN 55032 Class B (radiated & conducted); IEC 60730-1, 60730-2-9.  
- **CE** (European Economic Area); **UKCA** (UK Conformity Assessed) as required.

**DFR (Design for Reliability)**  
- Derate components (e.g. 50 %: 50 V cap on 12 V rail).  
- Conformal coating (e.g. acrylic) on every board after test (mandatory where specified).  
- Humidity sensor: PTFE filter cap.  
- No electrolytics → ceramic or polymer only (ceramic: Class 1 NP0, Class 2 X7R/X5R/Y5V; polymer: tantalum, aluminium, hybrid).  
- **Drop test:** e.g. 1.5 m onto concrete, all six faces (housing + PCB).  
- **Thermal:** e.g. −10 °C to +60 °C operating, 95 % RH non-condensing.  
- **HALT:** e.g. 85 °C / 85 % RH for 1000 h.

**Boundary and stress testing – where they fit**

| | When to **consider** (design / plan) | When to **execute** |
|---|--------------------------------------|---------------------|
| **Boundary test** | **Phase 3** – Define min/max limits in spec (voltage, temperature, frequency, data rate, humidity). DFR/DFE set the limits; schematic and layout must support them. | **DVT (Phase 5)** – Run tests at **spec limits** (min/max Vcc, min/max temp, edge of data rate, etc.) to verify the product works over the full specified range. Optional: late **EVT** for critical parameters. |
| **Stress test** | **Phase 3** – DFR defines stress conditions (HALT, drop, thermal cycling, voltage margin). Design and derating must allow survival in stress tests. | **DVT (Phase 5)** – Execute **stress tests** (environmental chamber, thermal cycling, HALT, drop, voltage margin) to find failure points and validate margins. **PVT (Phase 6)** can repeat key stress tests on pilot units to confirm production build. |

---

**Phase 4 – Prototyping & EVT**  
Order parts; fabricate PCB (e.g. Exception PCB, Newbury Electronics); assemble PCBA; generate and release Gerbers. Investigate issues; test basic functionality; check power management.

**Phase 5 – DVT (Design Verification Test)**  
**Boundary tests** (at spec limits: min/max voltage, temp, frequency, data rate). **Stress tests** (environmental chamber, thermal cycling, HALT, drop, voltage margin). EMI/EMC pre-compliance; battery life; sensor accuracy.

**Phase 6 – PVT (Production Verification Test)**  
Pilot run with contract manufacturer (e.g. 200–1000 units); set up jig; finalize fabrication and assembly files.

**Phase 7 – Mass Production (MP)**  
Support production; factory visits (e.g. 1–2×); supplier quality.

**Phase 8 – Sustaining & Next-Gen**  
Field failure analysis; firmware updates; cost reduction; new variant (e.g. Ireland/EU).

## 2. Signal Integrity

### 2.1 What Is Signal Integrity?

- **SI = signal’s ability to propagate without distortion** (quality of the signal on the transmission line).
- SI must be addressed in **PCB design**; fixes after fabrication are very limited.
- In digital circuits, signals are analog; they suffer noise, distortion, and loss.

### 2.2 Why It Matters

- With bad SI: intermittent operation, prototype-only success, lab vs field differences, sensitivity to production lot.
- **Signal has lost integrity when:** shape is distorted; unwanted noise degrades S/N; it disturbs other circuits.
- **Good SI when:** signals propagate without distortion; board is not susceptible to external noise/EMI per standards; board does not radiate beyond allowed EMI.

### 2.3 What Leads to SI Issues

**Main driver:** **Faster rise times** (and higher frequencies). At low/moderate speeds, PCB-related SI is rare; at high speeds it becomes critical.

**Categories of issues:**

- Impedance discontinuities (see §10)
- Reflections, ringing, overshoot, undershoot
- Crosstalk (mutual capacitance and inductance)
- Via stubs
- Skew and jitter
- Signal attenuation
- Ground bounce
- Power/ground distribution (PDN)
- EMI

**Reflections, ringing, overshoot, undershoot**

- **Reflection:** Part of the signal bounces at impedance changes. Causes distortion, overshoot, undershoot.
- **Reduce:** Constant impedance; good ground; series termination near source.
- **Ringing:** Oscillation (damped overshoot/undershoot) after a step. It appears when the line is long enough that a reflection can travel back to the driver and re-reflect (e.g. driver–receiver distance > λ/4), so energy bounces several times before settling.
  - **Why it matters:** (1) **More EMI** – the oscillating voltage/current radiates at the ringing frequency and harmonics. (2) **More current** – extra current flows during the ring, increasing stress and power. (3) **Worse performance** – the receiver may sample during the ring and see wrong levels or tighter timing margin; jitter increases. (4) **Sometimes audible** – in circuits with magnetics or ceramics, the ringing frequency can fall in the audio band and be heard as buzz or whine.
  - **How to reduce ringing:** Use **termination** so the line looks matched (series R at source ~ Z0 − R_driver, or parallel/ODT at load) and reflections are absorbed instead of bouncing. **Shorten the line** or **avoid long unterminated stubs** so there isn’t a λ/4 resonance. Keep a **continuous reference plane** under the trace so the return path is tight and impedance is stable. Add a **small series resistor** (e.g. 22–33 Ω for a 50 Ω line) near the driver to dampen the edge and reduce reflection magnitude. Prefer **differential signalling** and **microvias/back-drill** to remove via stubs that can ring. Avoid **T-branches** and multi-drop; use **daisy chain** or point-to-point.
- **Overshoot:** Transition above final high level; **undershoot:** below final low level (can go negative).
- **Reduce:** Termination (series at source, parallel/ODT at load); minimize via stubs; use microvias/HDI; daisy-chain instead of multi-drop; differential pairs (more tolerant of return-path issues).

**Real example:** USB 2.0 D+ trace is 90 Ω on top layer, then drops through a via to inner layer where the antipad is large → local impedance ~110 Ω. No series termination at the PHY. Result: reflection at the via and at the connector → eye closes, "USB device not recognized." Fix: keep pair on one layer, or size via for 90 Ω; add proper termination per PHY datasheet.

---

**Crosstalk**

- **Mutual capacitance:** dV/dt on aggressor → current in victim (electric field).
- **Mutual inductance:** dI/dt on aggressor → voltage in victim (magnetic field). With discontinuities, inductive coupling often dominates.
- **Reduce:** Increase spacing (energy ∝ 1/distance²); place trace close to ground plane; differential routing; route orthogonally on different layers; shorten parallel run length; wide, uniform return path; lower Dk; differential pairs.

**Real example:** DDR DQ0 and DQ1 routed 4 mil apart for 2 inches next to the DRAM. 800 MHz clock causes strong dI/dt on DQ0; victim DQ1 sees a ghost pulse. Result: intermittent bit errors during memory test. Fix: increase spacing to ≥3× trace width (e.g. 15 mil) or route on stripline with ground between; keep DQ group spacing per DDR layout guide.

---

**Via stub**

- Unused via barrel (e.g. from inner signal layer to bottom) acts as **resonator** at λ/4 → stores energy and attenuates that frequency.
- Use back-drill, blind/buried vias, or route on one layer to avoid stubs.

**Real example:** PCIe Gen3 on layer 3; via goes from L3 to bottom (L10). Stub L3–L10 is ~λ/4 at 4 GHz. Result: notch in insertion loss, link trains at Gen1 only. Fix: back-drill the stub from the back of the board, or use blind via from top to L3 only so no stub exists.

---

**Skew and jitter**

- **Skew:** Different arrival times (e.g. data vs clock) → sampling errors. Tighter at higher speeds.
- **Tip:** Minimize skew by **delay/length matching** (trace length matching).
  
- **jitter**
What it is: Variation in when a signal edge occurs—either in time or in phase. So the edge doesn’t always happen at the same instant; it “wobbles” in time. 
**Why it happens:** Noise, reflections, crosstalk, power noise, oscillator instability, etc. 
**Why it matters:** Jitter shrinks the valid sampling window and adds uncertainty to setup/hold. Too much jitter → bit errors, link training failures, or reduced margin. 
**Real example:** DDR3 DQS and DQ traces routed without length matching; DQ2 is 800 mil longer than DQS. At 1600 MT/s the setup margin is exceeded. Result: DDR training fails or random errors in OS. Fix: match DQ to DQS within the timing budget (e.g. ±20% of unit interval); add serpentines on the shorter traces, same layer for matched group.

For DDR, the **AC timing characteristics** table in the DRAM datasheet (clock period/jitter, DQ–DQS setup and hold times, allowed skew, flight‑time limits) defines this timing budget. PCB routing plus controller delay settings (read/write levelling) must keep **board delay and skew** within those numbers.

---

**Signal attenuation**

- Loss from conductor (skin effect) and dielectric (Df). Higher frequency → more loss → slower edges → detection errors.
- **Tip:** For attenuation-limited designs, choose **low-loss material** and control **trace geometry**.

**Real example:** 10 Gbps SERDES over 12-inch FR4 microstrip; Df and conductor loss cause 3 dB insertion loss at 5 GHz. Result: eye nearly closed at receiver, link drops. Fix: use low-loss laminate (e.g. mid-loss or low-loss), shorten trace, or use wider/shorter path; run channel simulation with vendor Dk/Df data.

---

**Ground bounce**

- PCB ground and die ground at different voltages during switching (shared inductance, dI/dt).
- **Reduce:** Decoupling to local ground; series current-limiting resistors; decoupling close to pins; solid ground.

**Real example:** 32-bit bus driver switches 24 lines simultaneously; shared bond wire and pin inductance ~5 nH. dI/dt = 50 mA/ns per line → large shared L×dI/dt. Result: ground pin bounces 200 mV; downstream receiver sees false level and misreads. Fix: add 0.1 µF + 10 nF decoupling very close to the driver ground pin; reduce simultaneous switching or add series R on non-critical lines.

---

**Power and ground distribution**

- Planes have finite impedance. Switching currents cause voltage drop → reduced noise margin or malfunction.
- **Reduce:** Power and ground planes close together; multiple low-inductance decoupling caps near device pins; short package leads.

**Real example:** FPGA core supply 1.0 V; single 10 µF cap 15 mm away; thin power trace and long via chain. During boot, current surge causes 150 mV droop on Vcore. Result: FPGA brown-out or unreliable boot. Fix: place multiple 0.1 µF/1 µF ceramics &lt;5 mm from Vcore pins; wide short traces and multiple vias to plane; ensure ZPDN &lt; target up to fm.

---

**EMI**

- Increases with frequency and faster rise times. Far-field: linear with frequency for single-ended, squared for differential.
- **Real example:** Product fails RE test at 500 MHz (clock harmonic). Clock on top layer with 2-inch loop between trace and discontinuous ground (slot for connector). Result: slot acts as slot antenna, radiates. Fix: continuous ground under clock; shorten loop; route clock on inner layer (stripline) or add ground stitching so return path is direct; consider series R to slow edge if timing allows.

---

## 3. Formulas (Velocity, Wavelength, Critical Length)

**Why:** To decide if a net is “electrically long” and needs transmission-line treatment (impedance control, termination, no stubs).

---

### 3.1 Propagation velocity

**Formula:** `v = c / √ε_eff`

| Symbol | Meaning | Typical |
|--------|---------|---------|
| **v** | Propagation velocity (e.g. in/ns) | — |
| **c** | Speed of light in free space | **11.8 in/ns** |
| **ε_eff** | Effective dielectric constant (from trace structure) | — |

**Typical v:** **Microstrip** (outer layer) ≈ 6.5–7.0 in/ns · **Stripline** (inner layer) ≈ 5.5–6.0 in/ns.

**Use:** Use this *v* in the wavelength and critical-length formulas below.

---

### 3.2 Wavelength

**Formula:** `λ = v / F_m`

| Symbol | Meaning |
|--------|---------|
| **λ** | Wavelength (same units as v, e.g. inches) |
| **v** | Propagation velocity (from above) |
| **F_m** | Highest significant frequency; from rise time: **F_m ≈ 0.35/T_r** or **0.5/T_r** |

**Rule:** If trace length **L ≥ λ/12** → treat as **transmission line** (controlled Z, termination, no stubs).

---

### 3.3 Critical length (rise-time rule)

**Formula:** `L_c = (v × T_r) / 6`

| Symbol | Meaning |
|--------|---------|
| **L_c** | Critical length (same units as v, e.g. inches) |
| **v** | Propagation velocity (e.g. 6 in/ns for stripline) |
| **T_r** | Rise time of the signal (e.g. in ns) |

**Rule:** If **trace length > L_c** → apply high-speed rules (impedance, termination, continuous reference, no stubs, length matching if needed).

**Example:** 50 MHz SPI, T_r = 1 ns, v = 6 in/ns → **L_c = 6 × 1 / 6 = 1 in**. If trace is 8 cm (≈3.15 in) → longer than 1 in → treat as high-speed.

---

### 3.4 Ground bounce (concept)

**Formula:** `V ≈ L × (dI/dt)`

| Symbol | Meaning |
|--------|---------|
| **V** | Voltage spike on ground at the chip |
| **L** | Inductance of shared power/ground path |
| **dI/dt** | Rate of change of current (faster switching → worse) |

**Use:** Reduce *L* (solid ground plane, short pins, decoupling close to pins) and limit simultaneous switching where possible.

## 4. PCB Transmission Lines and Controlled Impedance

### 4.1 What Is a PCB Transmission Line?

- **Two conductors:** signal trace + return (usually ground plane). Dielectric between them.
- At high frequencies the line has **impedance at every point**. Uniform geometry → uniform **characteristic impedance Z0**.
- **Controlled impedance** = keeping Z0 constant along the path (and terminating correctly) to avoid reflections and distortion.

### 4.2 When Is an Interconnection a Transmission Line?

- When **propagation time** from source to load is **not negligible** compared to **λ/4** or **rise time**.
- Low speed (<50 MHz, <20 Mbps): propagation time small → lumped analysis often OK.
- High speed: must treat as transmission line (impedance, termination, no stubs).

### 4.3 Structures

- **Microstrip:** Outer layer; trace over one plane; air + dielectric. Single-ended and differential.
- **Stripline:** Inner layer; trace between two planes; full dielectric. Single-ended and differential.
- **Coplanar:** Signal and ground on same layer (e.g. coplanar waveguide). Can combine with microstrip/stripline.
- **Important:** The **return path** (plane or coplanar conductor) is part of the transmission line.

### 4.4 How to Control Impedance

The factors that influence impedance are the **PCB material dielectric**, the **trace thickness**, **trace width**, and **height from the ground (reference) plane**. Controlling these in the stack-up and layout gives the target Z0.

- **Controlled dielectric:** Build to ±10% dielectric thickness; no explicit Z0 test.
- **Impedance control:** Control dielectric, trace width, spacing; verify with TDR coupons. Typical tolerance ±10% (Sierra ±5%).
- Impedance depends on: dielectric (Dk), trace thickness, width, height to reference.

### 4.5 Which Signals Need Controlled Impedance?

- Check **datasheets** for required Z0 (e.g. 50 Ω SE, 90/100 Ω diff).
- **Schematic:** Mark controlled-impedance nets; classify as differential (100/90/85 Ω) or single-ended (40/50/55/60/75 Ω). Use N/P suffix for diff pairs. Put layout rules in schematic or “Read me”.

### 4.6 Routing Differential Pairs

- Route **parallel**, **constant spacing**, **symmetric**. Minimize spacing change at pads/ends.
- **Tip:** Route differential pairs symmetrically; keep signals parallel.
- When changing reference plane, add **ground transition vias** between the two grounds.

### 4.7 Components, Vias, and Coupling Capacitors

- **Tip:** Do not place components or vias **between** differential pairs (causes discontinuity).
- **Tip:** Place coupling capacitors **symmetrically** (they are discontinuities).
- No vias/components between the pair even if routed around them symmetrically.
- Spacing between one diff pair and another: ≥ 5× trace width; keep-out to other signals 30 mils (50 mils for clocks).
- Minimize vias; if used, place symmetrically.
- **Tip:** Do not route high-speed signals at plane or PCB edges.

### 4.8 Length Matching

**Propagation delay** is the time it takes for a signal to travel through a PCB trace. Typical PCB delay is **150–180 ps per inch** (≈ 6–7 ps per mm). Longer traces = more delay. Delay mismatch between two traces = **skew**.

- Propagation delay ∝ length when W, T, H same → **match lengths** for delay matching.
- Match **differential pair** lengths closely (serpentine on shorter trace); match **group** lengths for buses.
- **Tip:** Keep same group on same layer to avoid skew (different layers → different speed).
- Serpentine: min spacing 4× trace width; segment length ≥ 1.5× width. Add length **at mismatch point** and **near bends**.
- **Tip:** Add length correction at the mismatching point; place length compensation close to bends.
- **how the serpentine must be consider:** Mismatch happens at **vias**, **bends**, and **breakouts**. Fix it **immediately at the source** (at that via, bend, or breakout). Do **not** fix it far away along the trace—serpentine is **local correction**, not **global decoration**. If you add length somewhere else, the two legs of the pair still run with unequal length over part of the path, which can cause skew and timing issues within that segment.
- If pair crosses layers with a bend, match **each segment** separately. **Tip:** Length differences must be compensated in each segment.
- Prefer **symmetric breakout** to avoid serpentines; small loops on shorter trace OK if space allows. **Tip:** Bends &lt;15 mm apart can compensate each other; no serpentine needed.
- Some tools include pad/via length in length; ensure equal pad entry and same via count for pairs.

### 4.9 Reference Layers (Return Path)

**Reference plane vs return plane**

- **Reference plane:** The metal layer (almost always **ground** or **power**) immediately next to the signal layer that we use to **define** the transmission line. It sets the **characteristic impedance** (e.g. 50 Ω, 90 Ω diff) and is the **voltage reference** the receiver uses. In stack-up formulas, “height to reference” means the dielectric distance from the trace to this plane.
- **Return plane:** The metal layer that carries the **return current** of the signal (the current that flows back to the source and closes the loop). At high frequency, return current does not take the shortest geometric path; it takes the **path of least inductance**, which is **directly under the signal trace**. So the return plane is the layer where that current actually flows.
- In most high-speed layouts **the same plane is both**: the ground (or power) layer adjacent to the signal is the reference for impedance and voltage, and it also carries the return current. We use “reference” when talking about impedance and stack-up, and “return” when talking about current path and EMI.

**Where we need them**

- **Reference plane:** Whenever we have **controlled impedance** (50 Ω, 90 Ω, 100 Ω, etc.) or a defined voltage reference; the stack-up is built with “height to reference” to that plane.
- **Return plane:** Whenever the net is **high-speed** (trace length &gt; critical length or protocol is DDR/USB/PCIe/HDMI, etc.); the signal needs a low-inductance return path so impedance is stable, loop area is small, and we avoid splits under the trace.

**How to use and consider them**

- **Solid plane next to signal:** Every high-speed signal layer should have a **solid** ground (or power) plane on an **adjacent** layer. That adjacent plane is both the reference and the return plane.
- **Keep the plane continuous:** Do **not** route high-speed traces over **splits**, **slots**, or **large voids**. If the plane is broken, return current must detour → large loop → more inductance, worse SI, more EMI.
- **3W rule:** Keep ground (reference) copper at least **3× trace width** on each side of the trace so the field and return path stay well defined.
- **When the signal changes layer (vias):** The return path must “jump” to the plane next to the new layer. Add **stitching vias** (ground vias) **close to** the signal via so return current can switch planes with minimal loop; for differential pairs, prefer one ground via between the two signal vias.
- **When reference is power on one side and ground on the other:** Use a **stitching capacitor** (10–100 nF) between the two planes near the signal path so high-frequency return current can cross (GND ↔ VCC) through the cap.
- **Power as reference:** If a power plane (e.g. 1.8 V) is the reference, we still need a return path for AC; decoupling caps between power and ground provide that. Place bypass caps **near** where the signal enters/exits that segment.
- **Stripline (inner layer):** The trace has **two** planes (above and below). Both act as reference and return; keep **both** solid and continuous under the trace.

**Summary**

| Term | Meaning | Where | How |
|------|---------|--------|-----|
| **Reference plane** | Plane that **defines** trace impedance and voltage reference (usually ground or power). | Next to every controlled-impedance / high-speed signal layer. | One solid plane per signal layer; no splits/slots under the trace; 3W rule. |
| **Return plane** | Plane that carries the **return current** of the signal (same loop as the trace). | Same as reference in most layouts (the adjacent ground or power). | Keep it **continuous** under the trace; add **stitching vias** at layer changes; use **stitching caps** when crossing between GND and VCC reference. |

---

- High-speed return current follows the signal path (path of least inductance). Need **continuous reference** next to signal layer.
- **Tip:** Avoid routing over split planes; route around split or use stitching.
- **3W rule:** Ground plane at least 3× trace width on each side.
- If crossing two reference planes: **stitching capacitor** (10nF–100nF) near signal path; or trace bridge between grounds under signal (preferred over capacitor when both are ground).
- **Tip:** Stitching capacitor needed when routing over split planes or plane obstructions.
- Slots in reference under trace: avoid; if unavoidable, use **stitching vias**.
- **Tip:** When signal changes ground reference, add stitching vias (preferably ground via between signal vias).

### 4.9.1 Via Stitching: Considerations and Placement

**Two main uses**

1. **High-speed signal return path** – Stitching vias next to a **signal via** (or along a slot) so return current can jump between ground layers with minimal loop. Goal: low inductance, continuous return path.
2. **RF / antenna ground fence** – A row (or ring) of vias along an **RF trace** or around an **antenna zone** to form a continuous ground wall. Goal: suppress cavity resonances, reduce radiation, isolate the RF region.

**Via size**

- **Electrically:** Diameter has little effect on shielding or return-path quality; what matters is **spacing** and **continuity**. One small via has higher inductance than one large via, but **multiple** vias in parallel lower the total inductance.
- **Practically:** Use the same size as other ground vias (e.g. 8–12 mil drill) or **smaller** if you need to pack many vias (e.g. 6–8 mil). Smaller vias cost more and need good aspect ratio; larger vias use more space. Typical: **8–12 mil** drill for stitching; match your fab’s standard where possible.

**Via spacing (what to calculate)**

- Spacing is based on **wavelength in the PCB**, not in free space. Use the **highest frequency** (or fastest rise-time equivalent frequency) of concern.

**Wavelength in PCB:**

`λ_PCB = v / f = c / (f × √εr)`

- **v** = propagation velocity on the layer (e.g. in/ns or mm/ns)  
- **f** = frequency (Hz) – use maximum significant frequency (e.g. from rise time: f_max ≈ 0.35/T_r)  
- **c** ≈ 3×10⁸ m/s, **εr** = relative permittivity of the dielectric (e.g. FR4 ≈ 4)

**Spacing rules (maximum distance between stitching vias):**

| Application | Rule | Purpose |
|-------------|------|---------|
| **RF ground fence / antenna** | **λ/20** | Prevent resonances, good isolation, &gt;40% EMI reduction in many cases |
| **RF board edge / general stitching** | **λ/8** | Limit parallel-plate modes, tie grounds together |
| **High-speed return path (EMI)** | **λ/20** (tighter) or **λ/10** (common max) | Keep return path low impedance, avoid ground resonances |

**Practical spacing by frequency (λ/20 in FR4, approximate):**

- **&gt;10 GHz:** 1–2 mm (very dense)
- **5–10 GHz:** 2–5 mm
- **1–5 GHz:** 5–10 mm
- **&lt;1 GHz:** 10–20 mm

You can always place vias **closer** than the rule; closer = better isolation and lower inductance, at the cost of space and fabricability.

**Where to put them**

| Situation | Where to place stitching vias |
|-----------|-------------------------------|
| **High-speed signal changes layer** | **Immediately next to** the signal via(s), same side or both sides; for a **differential pair**, place **one ground via between** the two signal vias so return current can switch planes with minimal loop. |
| **Trace over a slot or split** | Along **both sides** of the slot (or the split) so that the gap is “bridged” by vias and return current can cross. Space vias by λ/10–λ/20 along the slot. |
| **High-speed trace along a ground edge** | A row of vias along the **ground edge** (e.g. board edge or keep-out) at λ/10–λ/20 spacing so the edge does not act as an open boundary. |
| **RF trace or microstrip** | **Ground fence**: one or two **rows of vias** parallel to the trace, on both sides, spacing **λ/20**; vias connect all ground layers to form a wall. |
| **Antenna zone / RF block** | **Ring or fence** of vias **around** the antenna or RF area, spacing **λ/20**, to isolate from the rest of the board and reduce coupling. |
| **Two-layer board** | **Many** stitching vias between top and bottom ground pours so both sides are one continuous ground; spacing depends on highest frequency (e.g. 15–20 mm for &lt;1 GHz, tighter for multi-GHz). |

**How to place them**

- **At a signal via:** 1–3 ground vias **close** to the signal via (e.g. within 20–50 mil); for diff pairs, **one ground via between** the two signal vias, plus optional ones on the outside. Symmetric placement for differential.
- **Along a trace (fence):** Vias in a **row** on one or both sides of the trace, spaced by **λ/20** (or λ/10 as a looser option). Stagger two rows if one row cannot meet spacing (e.g. fab limit) and you need better isolation.
- **Around RF / antenna:** **Closed** ring or U-shaped fence around the zone; spacing **λ/20**; all vias tied to ground on all layers. No large gaps.
- **Board edge:** Stitching vias along the **perimeter** at λ/8–λ/10 (or λ/20 for noisy boards) to tie top and bottom grounds and reduce edge radiation.

**Summary**

- **Via size:** 8–12 mil typical; smaller OK for dense stitching; effectiveness depends on **spacing**, not diameter.
- **Via spacing:** Calculate **λ_PCB = v/f** (or c/(f√εr)); use **λ/20** for RF fence and strong EMI control, **λ/10** as a common maximum for high-speed; place closer if space allows.
- **High-speed signal:** Stitching **next to** the signal via at every layer change; ground via **between** the two vias of a diff pair.
- **RF / antenna:** **Fence** along trace or **ring** around zone, spacing **λ/20**, all vias to ground.

### 4.10 Errors to Avoid (Controlled Impedance)

- **Distinguish controlled-Z traces:** Use slightly different width (e.g. 5.1 or 4.9 mil vs 5 mil) so fab can identify them.
- **No routing over split/void:** Use solid reference; route around void or stitch.
- **Top/bottom layers:** Need complete ground on adjacent layer; inner layers can be power or other signals.

**Trace width and spacing in impedance control: Should / Shouldn’t (CAM and manufacturing)**

**SHOULD**

- Use **unique widths** for controlled-impedance traces so they are not the same as normal signal widths.
- Keep **normal (non-impedance) signals** at different widths (e.g. 5 mil, 6 mil) from any controlled-impedance width.
- Follow the **impedance table** (stack-up / fab notes) exactly for controlled-Z width and spacing.
- Make controlled-impedance widths **easy to identify in CAM review** so the manufacturer does not have to guess.
- Keep **differential pair spacing** unique too (e.g. if 90 Ω diff uses 6 mil width and 7 mil spacing, avoid using that same width/spacing for non-impedance pairs).

**SHOULDN’T**

- Route **normal signals** using the **same width** as impedance-controlled traces.
- Mix controlled and uncontrolled traces **visually** (same width everywhere) so the shop cannot tell them apart.
- Use **random widths** that overlap with impedance widths from your stack-up.
- Let the **manufacturer guess** which nets need impedance control.

**Real-world example (practical scenario)**

You design a USB 2.0 board:

- USB D+/D− require **90 Ω differential**, which gives width = 6.0 mil, spacing = 7.0 mil in your stack-up.
- Your normal MCU signals are also routed at **6.0 mil**.
- The PCB shop **cannot tell** which 6 mil traces are USB and which are normal.

So you change the **USB pair** to **6.1 mil width** (same spacing, or adjust per calculator so 90 Ω is kept). Now in CAM:

- All **6.1 mil** traces → impedance-controlled (USB).
- All **6.0 mil** traces → normal signals.

This prevents manufacturing errors and wrong impedance treatment.

**Do:** Mark controlled-Z in schematic; use ground (or solid power) on both sides of inner signal layers; add stitching vias when the signal changes reference plane; match differential pair lengths and match per segment when path has bends/vias; keep same layer for length-matched groups; use symmetric breakout and same via count for both legs of a pair.

**Don’t:** Route controlled-Z traces over splits, slots, or voids; place components or vias between the two traces of a differential pair; route high-speed at plane or PCB edge; use different layers for the two legs of a pair when length matching; forget to compensate length in each segment after a via or bend.

### 4.13 High-Speed Routing: Outer Layers or Inner Layers?

High‑speed signals behave as transmission lines, and their performance depends heavily on which PCB layer they are routed on. The choice between **outer layers** (microstrip) and **inner layers** (stripline) affects impedance, EMI, crosstalk, manufacturability, and signal integrity. Below are the main considerations for layer selection.

**1. Why layer choice matters**

High‑speed signals require:

- a **continuous reference plane** directly under the trace  
- stable **controlled impedance**  
- minimal **EMI radiation**  
- minimal **via transitions**  
- predictable **propagation delay**

Different layers provide different electrical environments.

**2. Routing on outer layers (top / bottom)**

*Advantages*
- Lower effective dielectric constant → **faster propagation**
- Easier impedance control
- Easy to probe and rework
- Fewer vias → fewer stubs
- Ideal for short, critical high‑speed paths

*Disadvantages*
- Higher EMI radiation (fields leak into air)
- More sensitive to external noise
- Requires careful ground pour for shielding

*Best use cases*
- USB 2.0 / USB 3.x
- PCIe
- HDMI / MIPI
- DDR clocks and strobes
- RF feedlines (BLE, WiFi, GPS)

---

**3. Routing on inner layers (stripline)**

*Advantages*
- Fully sandwiched between ground planes → **excellent shielding**
- Lowest EMI radiation
- Very stable impedance
- Best for dense routing (DDR data buses)

*Disadvantages*
- Slower propagation (higher dielectric constant)
- Requires vias to enter/exit → via stubs
- Harder to probe or rework
- More expensive stackup

*Best use cases*
- DDR3/DDR4/DDR5 data lines
- Sensitive differential pairs
- Automotive EMC‑critical designs
- High‑speed buses requiring shielding

---

**4. Why some guides say “Route High‑Speed on Top/Bottom”

Some simplified guides recommend outer layers because:

- Outer layers naturally have a **ground plane directly below** (L2 or L(n‑1))
- Impedance is easier to control
- Manufacturers prefer outer‑layer impedance control
- Inner layers require tighter stackup tolerances

However, **this is not a universal rule**.  
Modern high‑speed boards often use **inner layers** for EMI reduction.

---

**5. Real engineering considerations**

*A. Reference plane availability*
A high‑speed trace must have a **solid, continuous ground plane** directly under it.

- If L2 is solid ground → L1 is good for high‑speed  
- If L3 is solid ground → L2 is good for high‑speed  
- If a layer has no adjacent ground → **never route high‑speed there**

This is the most important rule.

---

*B. EMI requirements*
- Outer layers radiate more  
- Inner layers radiate less  

Automotive and RF designs often prefer **inner layers** for this reason.

---

*C. Via count*
Every layer transition adds:

- via inductance  
- impedance discontinuity  
- possible via stub  

Outer‑layer routing reduces via count.

---

*D. Impedance stability*
- Inner layers (stripline) → most stable  
- Outer layers (microstrip) → slightly more variation  

---

**6. Practical “Should / Shouldn’t”

*Should*
- Route high‑speed on layers with a **solid ground plane directly under them**
- Use **inner layers** for DDR data, PCIe, HDMI, MIPI
- Use **outer layers** for USB, RF, clocks, and short high‑speed runs
- Minimize layer transitions
- Keep impedance widths unique

*Shouldn’t*
- Route high‑speed on layers without a ground reference
- Route over split planes
- Put DDR data on outer layers (EMI risk)
- Use inner layers without checking stackup tolerances
- Use same width for controlled and uncontrolled traces

---

**7. One-sentence summary**

**Outer layers = faster and easier but noisier.  
Inner layers = cleaner and quieter but slower and harder.  
Always choose the layer with a solid ground plane under it.**

---


### 4.11 Controlled Impedance Checklist

- Mark controlled impedance in schematic.
- Diff pair length match tolerance ~20% of rise/fall time.
- Use high-data-rate connectors.
- For stripline: ground or solid power above/below/sides of pairs (return path, EMI).

### 4.12 Stack-Up Mistakes (Controlled Impedance)

- Prepreg: avoid &gt;3 types; each &lt;10 mils; avoid very low-resin/high-glass (e.g. 7628, 2116).
- Diff pair spacing ≤ 2× trace width (e.g. 4 mil trace ≤ 8 mil space).
- Trace width ≤ 2× dielectric height to reference.

## 5. PCB Stackup Design

### 5.1 Construction and Basics

- Stack-up = order of cores, prepregs, foils. Usually **symmetrical**. Common thickness ~62 mil.
- High-speed: multilayer with **solid ground/power planes**; short device connections; low-inductance return for high-speed signals.

### 5.2 Best Way for High-Speed Signals

- **Aspect ratio:** 1:10 through-hole, 1:0.75 microvia.
- Prefer **microvias** to reduce thickness; ≤3 lamination cycles (avoid 5–6).
- Many blind-via connections → consider **back-drill** to limit lamination/drill cycles.
- 3 mil lines → 9 μm start copper.
- BGA pitch drives stack-up: 0.5 mm may use through-hole fanout; 0.4 mm usually blind/buried and more layers (e.g. ≥10 with via-in-pad).

### 5.3 Lamination and Materials

- **FR4:** Economical; OK below ~2.5–3 GHz; Dk can vary with frequency.
- **Rogers (e.g. RO4350):** RF; Dk flatter with frequency (e.g. 3.5 to 15 GHz).
- **Propagation velocity:** v ∝ 1/√εr → material and frequency matter for delay and distortion.

### 5.4 Planning High-Speed Stackup

- **Signal layer** adjacent to a **plane**; tight coupling (&lt;10 mil) to that plane.
- Plane (power or ground) can be return path; high-frequency current follows path of least inductance.
- **Ideal:** Signal layers separated by ground/power; dedicated, continuous ground and power planes; avoid signal–signal adjacency without plane between.

### 5.5 Material Categories (Loss vs Frequency)

- **Normal (e.g. FR-4):** Higher loss, Dk not flat → limited to few GHz.
- **Medium (e.g. Nelco N7000-2):** ~Half loss, flatter Dk → up to ~10 GHz.
- **High-speed low-loss (e.g. Isola I-Speed):** Lower loss, less noise.
- **RF/microwave (e.g. Isola Tachyon, Rogers, Taconic):** Flattest Dk, lowest loss → to ~20 GHz.
- Cost vs performance: less lossy = more expensive.

### 5.6 Fibre Weave Effect

- Glass weave causes **local Dk variation** → differential impedance variation (fibre weave effect). Tighter weave → more uniform Dk and Z0.
- For diff pairs, if one trace is in weave and the other in gap, Z0 differs → worse at high speed.

### 5.7 Terms

- **Dk (εr):** Relative permittivity; charge storage vs vacuum.
- **Df (tan δ):** Dissipation factor; loss rate.
- **CTE:** Coefficient of thermal expansion.
- **TCDk, TCDf:** Thermal coefficient of Dk and Df (stability with temperature).

### 5.8 Stackup: What to Do and What Not to Do

**Do:** Place each signal layer adjacent to a plane; keep signal-to-plane coupling tight (&lt;10 mil); use symmetrical stack; limit to ≤3 prepreg types and &lt;10 mil per prepreg; choose material (FR4 vs low-loss) based on frequency and loss budget; consider fibre weave for differential pairs (tighter weave = more uniform Z); plan layer count from BGA pitch and routing density.

**Don’t:** Stack two signal layers next to each other without a plane between; use &gt;3 lamination cycles if avoidable; ignore BGA pitch when choosing layer count; use very low-resin/high-glass prepregs (e.g. 7628, 2116) without considering resin starvation.

## 6. EMI and EMC

### 6.1 Goals

- **EMC (electromagnetic compatibility):** Two objectives—(1) product does **not generate** EMI beyond acceptable levels; (2) product is **immune** to specified levels of external EMI. If both are met, EMC is achieved.
- **Standards** define acceptable limits for both emission and susceptibility. Design for EMC from the start if the product must be EMC-certifiable.
- **FCC (U.S.):** Class A (business), Class B (residential); Class B is stricter.
- Address EMC in **design**; fixing after production is difficult and costly.

### 6.2 Sources of EMI

- **Typical sources:** RF signals and harmonics; fast signal transitions and high-frequency clocks; transients (lightning, power circuits, motors, breakers, power switching).
- **Conducted** (&lt;30 MHz): via power input lines, cables—predominant below 30 MHz.
- **Radiated** (&gt;30 MHz): from power/communication lines, switching devices, arcing, electrostatic discharge—predominant above 30 MHz.
- **Why it matters:** EMI increases with **frequency** and **faster rise times**. Far-field radiation: roughly **linear with frequency** for single-ended currents, **squared** for differential (common-mode). Large **loop area** and **slot antennas** (splits, gaps) make radiation worse.

### 6.3 Tips to Reduce EMI

**Loop area and return path**

- **Most EMI problems trace to bad grounding.** Good ground design reduces both emission and susceptibility.
- **Minimize current loop area.** Radiated EMI ∝ loop area × frequency². Every signal has a return path; at RF the return follows the path of least inductance (directly under the signal when a solid plane exists).
- Use a **solid, continuous ground plane** under high-speed traces so return current stays under the signal; pay special attention to high-frequency signals.
- **Avoid slots, splits, and gaps** under high-speed or high-current traces; they force the return to detour and create a large loop (slot antenna).
- Route high-speed signals on **inner layers (stripline)** when possible to contain fields between planes.
- Use **ground stitching vias** near high-speed traces and at layer changes so return current can follow with minimal loop area.

**Ground and power**

- Use **at least one full ground plane** (4-layer minimum for serious high-speed); prefer two ground planes in 6-layer stack for lower return-path inductance.
- **Do not split ground** unless necessary (e.g. safety/chassis); if split, connect at **one point only** and route crossing traces only over that point to avoid loop antennas.
- **Bypass and decoupling caps** should connect **directly to the ground plane** with short, low-inductance paths (no long traces, dedicated vias).
- Keep **power and ground planes tightly coupled** (thin dielectric) to increase interplane capacitance and reduce high-frequency impedance.

**Component placement and zoning**

- **Zone the board:** Keep noisy circuits (DC-DC, digital clocks, switching logic) **away** from sensitive analog and RF. Consider capacitive (E-field), inductive (H-field), and conducted (common-impedance) coupling.
- Place **connectors** so they do not form efficient antennas: avoid sharing a long common return path with a noise source between connector and IC (dipole effect).
- Put **sensitive high-speed devices** toward the **center** of the board, not at the edge (edge has different impedance and more exposure to external fields).

**Routing and topology**

- Use **differential pairs** for high-speed links; keep pairs **tight and symmetric** to minimize common-mode radiation.
- **Short, direct traces** radiate less than long meandering ones.
- **Impedance matching** and **termination** reduce ringing and overshoot → less harmonic content and less EMI.
- **Logic family:** Do not choose faster than required (faster logic generates more EMI). Prefer the **highest noise margin** for better immunity to received EMI.

**Filtering and shielding**

- **Filters at I/O:** Place **differential or common-mode filters** at **board input and output** (cables are the main radiators). Use 100–200 nH inductors at inputs; **ferrite beads** (effective in 100–300 MHz) with 1–100 nF caps at outputs after the main LC filter.
- **Ferrite on power input:** Series ferrite on power rails is an effective low-pass filter (high Z at HF, low Z at DC). A capacitor in parallel with the rail completes the filter.
- **Other EMC filtering options:** Mains filters; I/O filters; feedthrough capacitors; three-terminal capacitors; proper **cable shield terminations** (e.g. 360° at connector to avoid antenna effect).
- **Shielding:** Shield very high-frequency or sensitive circuit segments with **metal enclosures** (EMI cans) when required.

**Stack-up and layers**

- Route critical high-speed signals on **at most 2 layers** referenced to the **same** plane to control return path.
- Ensure **every signal layer** has an **adjacent solid return plane** (ground or power).
- Use **enough vias** between ground planes to keep them at the same potential at high frequency.

### 6.4 What to Do and What Not to Do (EMI)

**Do**

- Use a solid, continuous ground plane; minimize loop area for every high-speed and high-current path.
- Place decoupling and bypass caps close to pins with short, direct connections to ground/power planes.
- Zone the layout: separate noisy and sensitive circuits; place connectors to avoid dipole/antenna structures.
- Route high-speed on inner layers (stripline) or with unbroken ground underneath; use ground stitching at layer changes.
- Apply impedance control and termination to reduce ringing and overshoot.
- Add filters (ferrite + cap) at power and signal I/O; use shielding where needed.
- Choose the slowest logic family that meets timing; avoid unnecessarily fast edges.
- Document EMC requirements and design for them from the first layout iteration.

**Don’t**

- Split or slot the ground under high-speed or high-current traces; don’t create long return detours.
- Route high-speed traces at the board edge or over plane splits/gaps without stitching or routing around.
- Share capacitor vias between multiple caps or use long traces to connect decoupling to pins.
- Place clocks or switching nodes next to connectors or sensitive inputs without spacing or shielding.
- Ignore EMC until the first test fail; fixing in layout is much cheaper than adding filters and shields later.
- Use logic with rise times faster than the application needs.

## 7. Power Integrity

**Ultra-short summary (the part you actually need)**  
- **Power Integrity** = clean, stable power for chips.  
- **PDN** = the whole power delivery path: VRM → planes → vias → caps → chip.  
- **Bad PI** = noise, resets, crashes, EMI.  
- **Good PI** = low impedance, good decoupling, solid planes, short vias.

### 7.1 PDN and Noise

- **PDN:** VRM → bulk caps → planes/vias/traces → ceramic decoupling → device pins (and package/die).
- **Noise:** Vnoise = L×(ΔI/tr). Larger ΔI, shorter tr, or larger L → worse. Many simultaneous switchers → n×ΔI.
- **L sources:** Loops in PCB (trace, via, plane, spreading), capacitor ESL/ESR, package and die (beyond PCB control).

### 7.2 Target Impedance

- **ZT-PDN = (VCC × Ripple%) / (0.5 × Imax).** Require ZPDN ≤ ZT over DC to fm (fm ≈ 0.5/tr).
- Typical ZT ~0.04–0.1 Ω; bandwidth DC–1 GHz (or more).
- **Design steps:** (1) Get fm from tr; (2) Compute ZT per rail; (3) Model PDN (SPICE); (4) Choose caps and geometry so ZPDN ≤ ZT.

### 7.3 Components

- **VRM:** Low Z at low frequency (e.g. &lt;ZT/2 at 1 kHz).
- **Bulk (electrolytic/tantalum/polymer):** Resonant ~kHz–100 kHz; choose C and ESR so Z &lt; ZT in that range; avoid very low ESR tantalum (resonant peaks with ceramics).
- **Ceramic (MLCC):** Resonant ~100 kHz–100 MHz; place **close** to IC; minimize via + trace loop inductance; use 3 different values in parallel to avoid high peaks (not just two).
- **Via/trace/plane inductance:** Larger via diameter, smaller via spacing, smaller plane separation and via height → lower L. Short, wide traces; caps as close as possible to pins.
- **Plane capacitance:** Helps at high frequency (e.g. 100–200 MHz); C ∝ εr×A/h.

### 7.4 Relation to SI and EMI

- Low PDN Z needs thin dielectrics and short paths → also helps crosstalk and SI. Controlling PDN noise reduces coupling to signals and EMI.

### 7.5 Power Integrity: What to Do and What Not to Do

**Do:** Place decoupling caps as close as possible to IC power/ground pins; use short, wide traces from cap to via to plane; use at least three different capacitor values in parallel to avoid resonant peaks; connect each cap with its own vias to power and ground; size bulk and ceramic caps so Z &lt; ZT over the required band; use multiple vias to planes to reduce via inductance; keep power/ground plane separation small for high interplane capacitance.

**Don’t:** Share one via between multiple decoupling capacitors; use long thin traces to connect caps to the IC; parallel only two different cap values (increases peak risk); place bulk caps far from the device; ignore loop inductance (via + trace) when placing ceramics; leave PDN design until after layout.

## 8. High-Speed Layout Design

### 8.1 Footprint and Pads

- **Pad size:** Standard +30% for manufacturability; for high-speed reduce to ~+5% to cut parasitic C and connection length (confirm fab capability).
- **Rounded pad corners** → route closer to pads → shorter connections.
- Via placement: close to pad (or in-pad) to reduce parasitics; may need DRC changes.

### 8.2 Component Placement

- **Floorplan first:** Blocks (power, RF, digital, analog); short connections; routing channels; power/ground needs. Place bypass and termination early.
- **Group by function and supply:** Same VCC/GND together; main chips then crystals, decoupling, termination per datasheet.
- **Avoid:** Sensitive high-speed at **board edge** (impedance, EMI); large parts blocking airflow to hot devices.
- **Termination:** **Parallel** – resistor as close as possible to **receiver** pin (min stub). **Series** – resistor inline right after **driver**.
- **Orientation:** Align similar parts for routing and assembly.
- **SMD** on one side; **through-hole** on top to simplify assembly.
- **Vias:** Size and depth matter for high-speed; smaller via often better; place close to pad (or in-pad).
- **Sockets:** Minimize (add inductance). Choose package for short traces; optimize thermal (e.g. thermal pad to plane).
- **Analog vs digital:** Place so return currents do not mix; isolate grounds if needed (single-point tie or virtual split).
- **Ground isolation:** Isolate board GND from chassis at connectors (e.g. USB, RJ45) when needed.
- **Surge suppressors:** Place near **connector**; avoid vias between suppressor and connector (inductance).

### 8.3 Routing – Ground and Power Planes

- **Unbroken ground** under top/bottom; inner signal layers with ground on both sides.
- **At least 4 layers** for serious high-speed; one solid ground plane (no traces in it); one power layer with large islands (3.3 V, 1.8 V, 5 V, etc.).
- **Two-layer:** Full ground under critical area; route mostly one side; many **ground stitching vias** between sides. Avoid split ground unless necessary (e.g. ESD); if split, cross only at the single tie point.
- **Via voids:** Stagger vias so plane can feed between them; avoid hot spots. **Rule:** ~15 mil spacing between vias where possible.

### 8.4 Polygon Voids

- Avoid **hot spots** (high current density between vias); allow space for plane current.

### 8.5 Trace Bend Geometry

- Prefer **135°** bends over 90°. Serpentine: min spacing **4× width**; segment **≥1.5× width** (DRC often does not check same-net spacing).

### 8.6 Signal Proximity and Stubs

- **Spacing:** Increase beyond minimum where possible to reduce crosstalk; widen spacing after bottlenecks.
- **Stubs:** Long stubs → antenna, reflections. Route **daisy chain**; avoid long branches (e.g. pull-up/pull-down on high-speed nets).

### 8.7 Differential Pairs

- **Constant spacing**, **symmetric** routing; minimize pad entry asymmetry.
- **No components or vias between** the two lines. **Same layer** and **same via count** for both legs.
- **Coupling caps:** Symmetric; prefer 0402, 0603 OK; avoid 0805/C-packs.
- **Length match:** Serpentine at mismatch and near bends; match per segment when path has vias/bends; prefer same layer for matched groups.

### 8.8 Return Path

- **Do not route over split** or void; route around. If crossing two reference planes, use **stitching capacitor** (10nF–100nF) or ground bridge near path.
- **Stitching vias** when signal changes layer (return changes plane); for diff pairs, place symmetrically.
- **Ground vias** at **source and load** so return uses the plane (not a top-layer trace).
- **Power as reference:** Avoid if possible; if used, stitching caps at source and load (or bypass caps near entry/exit if same rail).
- **Do not route** high-speed at **plane edge** or **board edge**.

### 8.9 Split and Virtual Split Ground

- **Split ground:** Analog under analog, digital under digital; tie at one point (e.g. near IC); do not route digital over analog ground or analog over digital ground.
- **Virtual split:** Same net; imaginary line; place and route so currents do not cross the virtual boundary.
- **Preferred:** One solid ground with good placement.

### 8.10 Component Selection for High-Speed

- **SMD** preferred (shorter, more stable). Package width close to **50 Ω trace width** reduces mismatch.
- **Tip:** Separate digital and analog supplies (e.g. ferrite); decouple successive high-frequency stages to avoid oscillation.

### 8.11 Layout: What to Do and What Not to Do

**Do:** Floorplan before placement; place termination and decoupling early; use 135° bends for high-speed; route differential pairs symmetrically with constant spacing; add ground vias at driver and receiver; use daisy chain instead of stubs; match lengths per segment and near bends; route around splits or add stitching; keep high-speed away from board and plane edges; align similar components; document controlled impedance and stack-up in fab notes.

**Don’t:** Route high-speed over splits, slots, or voids; use 90° bends on critical nets; put components or vias between diff pairs; leave long stubs (e.g. pull-up on high-speed net); route at plane/board edge; place sensitive parts at board edge; forget stitching when crossing reference planes or changing layers; skip length matching for timing-critical groups; mix analog and digital return paths under each other without isolation or single-point tie.

## 9. Vias and Microvias

### 9.1 Types

- **Through-hole:** Top to bottom; PTH/NPTH.
- **Blind:** Outer to inner; one end not visible after lamination.
- **Buried:** Inner to inner only.
- **Microvia:** Small (e.g. &lt;6 mil), laser; often 1–2 layers deep; **stacked** (one on top of other) or **staggered** (offset); **skipvia** passes through a layer without contact.
- **Via-in-pad (VIPPO):** Via under pad; filled and capped; short path, good for BGA; back-drill to remove stub when needed.

### 9.2 Design Tips

- **Prefer microvias** where applicable; **staggered** over stacked (stacked need fill/planarize).
- **Aspect ratio** minimum → better electrical and SI (less noise, crosstalk, EMI).
- **Smaller vias** → less stray C/L, better for HDI.
- **Via-in-pad:** Fill (except thermal pads); BGA matrix vias fill and planarize.
- **Thermal pads (e.g. QFN):** Vias in pad help solder flow and mechanical stability; if no vias, assembly may use stencil relief.
- **Documentation:** Drill file (tool codes, X-Y); fab drill chart (symbols, finished sizes, tolerances); Gerber via plugging if required; controlled depth for blind/buried.
- **Tolerances (examples):** Annular ring per IPC; drill-to-plane 8 mil; diameter ±3 mil; location 1 mil; solder mask clearance 2.5 mil beyond pad; antipad ~16 mil beyond hole (relief 8 mil).

### 9.3 Current and Microvia Design

- Via current ∝ surface area (π×D×board thickness, plating thickness). Example: 8 mil drill, 5 °C rise → ~0.8 A.
- **Microvias:** Small C/L; use **upper layers** for high-priority/high-transient supplies; place ground adjacent to high-transient power planes.

### 9.4 Vias: What to Do and What Not to Do

**Do:** Use back-drill or blind/buried vias to remove stubs on high-speed nets; prefer staggered over stacked microvias when possible; keep aspect ratio low for reliability and SI; fill and planarize via-in-pad under BGA; document drill chart, tolerances, and via plugging in fab; add ground stitching vias next to signal vias at layer change; use smaller vias where HDI allows to reduce parasitics.

**Don’t:** Leave long through-hole via stubs on clocks or high-speed serial links; use stacked microvias when staggered can be used (cost and process); route critical nets with many vias; forget to back-drill or switch to blind via when stub causes resonance; skip fill/planarize on via-in-pad in BGA area (solder joint risk).

## 10. Where Reflections Happen (Discontinuities)

Reflections occur at **impedance or path changes**:

1. **Via** – Adds L/C, changes Z.
2. **Branch (T/Y)** – Stubs, split impedance.
3. **Return path** – Split, gap, slot → detour, large loop, L, EMI, jitter.
4. **Stub** – Length ~λ/4 → resonance, strong reflection.
5. **Source** – Driver Z ≠ Z0 → use series termination.
6. **Load** – Receiver Z ≠ Z0 → parallel/ODT.
7. **Connector** – Parasitics, mismatch.

**Examples:**

- **DDR clock:** Via from L3 to L10 → stub L3–L10 → resonance, jitter, training fail. **Fix:** Back-drill, blind via, or route on one layer; solid reference; return vias near signal via.
- **USB D+/D−:** 90 Ω diff; vias and connector change Z → eye closure. **Fix:** Minimize vias; control Z through via (pad, antipad, spacing); short symmetric via section; 90 Ω connector, short lead-in; no stubs (test points in series if needed).

**Good practice:** Continuous ground under high-speed; **fly-by** for DDR (no T-branch); minimize vias, back-drill or blind/buried; termination (series/parallel/ODT); avoid λ/4 stubs; short, matched connector region.

**Discontinuities: What to Do and What Not to Do**

**Do:** Keep a continuous reference plane under high-speed nets; use fly-by (daisy chain) for DDR address/command/clock; minimize vias on critical nets and use back-drill or blind/buried to remove stubs; add series termination at source and/or parallel/ODT at load when required; route around splits or add stitching; keep connector region short and matched; follow connector vendor’s layout guide; ensure stub length ≪ λ/4 when a stub is unavoidable.

**Don’t:** Route high-speed over plane splits, gaps, or slots; use T-branch or star topology for DDR address/command/clock; leave long via stubs on clocks or high-speed serial; leave high-speed lines unterminated when length &gt; critical length; ignore connector parasitics and impedance; add test points or branches that form long stubs on high-speed nets.

## 11. When Is a Net High-Speed?

- **Rule:** Trace length &gt; **Lc = v×Tr/6** → high-speed (see §3).
- **By interface:**  
  **Low-speed (relaxed):** I²C, UART &lt;3 Mbps, GPIO, slow SPI (&lt;10–20 MHz).  
  **High-speed (SI rules):** DDR2/3/4/5, PCIe, USB 2/3, HDMI, MIPI, GbE, fast SPI (&gt;40–50 MHz), RF (BLE, Wi‑Fi).

---

## 12. Design Rules Summary

**Do:** Controlled impedance (50 Ω SE, 90–100 Ω diff as spec’d); continuous reference under high-speed; correct termination and length matching; minimize vias, back-drill/blind/buried for critical nets; fly-by for DDR address/command; tight, equal diff pair spacing and length; short return path; separate clocks from sensitive nets.

**Don’t:** Route high-speed over splits/gaps; use T-branch for DDR; leave long via stubs; leave lines unterminated when spec says otherwise; ignore length match for timing groups; put components/vias between diff pairs; route at plane/board edge.

**Low-speed nets (I²C, UART, GPIO, slow SPI):** No impedance control, length match, or diff rules; vias and routing relaxed.

---

## 13. Per-Interface Requirements

- **DDR:** Stripline, 50 Ω; fly-by; DQ/DQS length match; ODT; no split under CK/DQS → avoid training fail.
- **USB 2.0:** 90 Ω diff; few vias; constant spacing/width → avoid random disconnect.
- **High-speed SPI:** Short traces or series termination; match clock/data length if needed → avoid double clock, corruption.
- **RF (BLE, Wi‑Fi):** 50 Ω; continuous GND; short straight path → avoid range drop.
- **PCIe:** Minimal vias, no stubs; back-drill on thick boards → avoid link down to lower Gen.

## 14. High-Speed PCB Design Checklist (Appendix)

**Layout**

1. High-speed traces on solid planes; do not cross splits.
2. Top/bottom controlled-Z: ground reference each side.
3. Inner controlled-Z: ground both sides.
4. Ground/power vias near component pad, short traces.
5. Minimize ground current loops.
6. Call out controlled impedance in fab notes.

**Board and dimensions**

7. Board dimensions; hole-to-hole and mounting from board edge.
8. Test point labels; signal names on connector pins where possible.
9. Legend: one or two directions; size e.g. 30/6 mil (25/4 min).
10. Polarity on polarized parts; pin 1 on all ICs.

**Fab drawing**

11. Fab notes: IPC Class 2/3 (Class 2 = most commercial; Class 3 = high-reliability/ safety‑critical); layer-wise impedance; solder mask color; annular ring/drill min.
12. Stack-up: thickness; copper per layer; material and thickness; blind/buried notes; via-in-pad note for BGA; ≥3 fiducials.
13. SMD: solder mask and paste defined; through-hole: mask openings.
14. QFN center pad: paste in small patches.
15. Drill chart: separate symbol per size (+ via, square PTH, triangle NPTH); PTH ±3 mil, NPTH ±2 mil; dates, design name; all drills in chart.

**Design and output**

16. Clear all valid DRCs; 100% routing.
17. Fab layer notes and impedance vs stack-up consistent; Gerbers.
18. GC Prevue: no silvers, no hanging nets; SMB/SPB, SMT/SPT match; copper pours: spacing, no shorts.
19. BOM validated.
20. Docs: assembly top/bottom, fab, drill, all layers, schematic PDF.
21. Cutout: on outline and fab; dimension; outline on all Gerbers; outline 2 mil; verify in Gerbers.
22. Deliverables: .art, art_aper, art_param, nc_param, .drl, placement, IPC netlist, ODB++, CAD on request.

## 15. Glossary

- **Reference plane** – Metal layer (ground or power) adjacent to the signal layer that defines characteristic impedance and voltage reference; same layer usually also carries return current.
- **Return plane** – Metal layer that carries the signal’s return current; at high frequency this flows directly under the trace (path of least inductance). See §4.9.
- **Propagation delay** – Time for a signal to travel through a PCB trace. Typical: 150–180 ps/in (≈ 6–7 ps/mm). Longer trace = more delay; delay mismatch between traces = skew. See §4.8.
- **Flight time** – Same idea as propagation delay; delay along trace (e.g. ~150 ps/in).
- **Skew** – Arrival-time difference between related signals (e.g. DQS vs DQ). Caused by propagation-delay mismatch between traces.
- **Timing budget** – Allowable delay/skew before setup/hold fail.
- **AC timing characteristics (DDR)** – Datasheet timing rules (clock period/jitter, setup/hold, read/write delays, DQS timing, flight-time/skew limits) that define how fast DDR signals may toggle and when they must be valid so the DRAM and controller stay synchronized.
- **Setup time (tSU)** – Time a signal must be stable **before** the sampling clock/strobe edge.
- **Hold time (tH)** – Time a signal must remain stable **after** the sampling clock/strobe edge.
- **ODT** – On-die termination.
- **Ground bounce / SSN** – Local ground shift when many outputs switch (L, dI/dt).
- **S-parameters (S11, S21)** – Reflection/transmission in frequency domain.
- **Insertion loss** – Power lost through channel; too much → small eye.
- **Return loss** – Power reflected; less reflection = better.
- **Fly-by** – Controller → DRAM1 → DRAM2 → … (no stubs).
- **Via stub** – Unused via barrel beyond signal layer → resonator.
- **Via stitching** – Ground vias placed to connect ground layers (return path at layer change, along slots, or as RF fence); spacing often λ/10–λ/20. See §4.9.1.
- **IPC Class 1 / Class 2 / Class 3** – PCB reliability levels per IPC‑6011/6012. Class 1: general consumer (toys, simple gadgets). Class 2: dedicated service (most commercial/industrial). Class 3: high‑reliability/safety‑critical (automotive safety, medical, aerospace, telecom).
- **Fabrication Class A / B / C** – Manufacturer capability levels (feature size), not safety. Class A: standard capability; Class B: advanced (smaller drill/trace/space); Class C: HDI/microvia and via‑in‑pad capability.

## 18. DFM notes

### Prepreg Rules for High‑Speed PCB Stackups (Short & Practical)

High‑speed PCB stackups depend heavily on the choice and quality of **prepregs**.  
Prepregs determine dielectric thickness, impedance stability, and lamination quality.

This document summarizes the essential rules and their meaning.

---

### What Prepregs Are
Prepregs are the insulating layers between copper foils or cores.  
They are made of:
- fiberglass cloth (the “glass”)
- epoxy resin (the glue that bonds layers during lamination)

Different prepregs have different resin content, glass styles, and thicknesses.

---

### Key Rules and What They Mean

**1. Use No More Than Three Prepreg Types**  
Using many different prepreg types causes:
- inconsistent shrinkage during lamination  
- unpredictable final thickness  
- unstable impedance  

**Meaning:**  
Keep the stackup simple and consistent.  
Use **1–3 prepreg types maximum**.

---

**2. Each Prepreg Layer Should Be < 10 mil Thick**  
Thick prepregs:
- shrink more during lamination  
- create large thickness variation  
- cause impedance drift  

**Meaning:**  
Use **thin prepregs** (typically 2–8 mil) for stable impedance.

---

**3. Avoid Low‑Resin, High‑Glass Prepregs (e.g., 7628, 2116)**  
Low‑resin prepregs can suffer from:
- resin starvation  
- voids  
- delamination  
- poor bonding  
- warpage  
- impedance variation  

**Meaning:**  
Avoid **7628** and **2116** in high‑speed designs.  
Prefer **1080, 2113, 3313, 106** (higher resin content).

---

### Why These Rules Matter
High‑speed signals (USB, DDR, PCIe, HDMI, RF) require:
- tight impedance tolerance  
- stable dielectric thickness  
- uniform resin distribution  
- reliable lamination  

Bad prepreg choices lead to:
- reflections  
- skew  
- crosstalk  
- eye‑diagram collapse  
- board failure in reflow  

---

### Should / Shouldn’t Summary

*Should*
- Use **1–3 prepreg types**  
- Use **thin prepregs** (< 10 mil)  
- Use **high‑resin prepregs** (1080, 2113, 3313)  
- Keep stackup symmetrical  
- Work with your PCB fabricator to lock the stackup  

*Shouldn’t*
- Use **7628** or **2116** for high‑speed  
- Use **thick prepregs** (> 10 mil)  
- Mix many prepreg types  
- Allow uncontrolled resin flow  
- Build asymmetrical stackups  

---

### One‑Sentence Summary
Use thin, high‑resin prepregs and keep the stackup simple to ensure stable impedance and reliable high‑speed performance.

---

### High‑Speed PCB Design: Via Rules, Stackup Tips, and Microvia Basics

This document summarizes the essential manufacturing and stackup rules for high‑speed PCB design, with short explanations of what each rule means and why it matters.

---

**1. Via and Lamination Design Rules**

**Minimum Aspect Ratio**
- **Through‑hole vias: 1:10**
- **Microvias: 1:0.75**

**Meaning:** A via cannot be too deep compared to its drill diameter. If the aspect ratio is too high, plating becomes unreliable and the via barrel can crack.

---

**Use Microvias to Reduce Board Thickness**  
**Meaning:** Microvias only go between adjacent layers, so the board does not need thick dielectric layers. This reduces total thickness and improves high‑speed performance.

---

**Limit Sequential Laminations (Max 3)**  
**Meaning:** Each lamination cycle adds risk of: delamination, resin starvation, misregistration. More than **5–6 laminations** becomes unreliable for manufacturing.

---

**Use Backdrilling When Many Blind Vias Exist**  
**Meaning:** Backdrilling removes unused via stubs that cause reflections in high‑speed signals. This improves signal integrity for DDR, PCIe, USB, etc.

---

**For 3‑mil Traces, Use 9 µm Start Copper**  
**Meaning:** Very fine traces require thin copper so the etching process stays accurate and impedance stays stable.

---

**2. Choosing the Best Stackup for High‑Speed Signals**

**BGA Pitch Determines the Stackup**
- **0.8 mm pitch:** through‑hole vias may work
- **0.5 mm pitch:** microvias recommended
- **0.4 mm pitch:** blind/buried vias and via‑in‑pad required

**Meaning:** The smaller the BGA pitch, the more advanced the via technology and the more layers you need.

---

**BGA Size Also Affects Layer Count**  
Large BGAs (9×9, 10×10, etc.) require: more routing channels, more escape layers, more power/ground layers.

**Meaning:** Bigger BGAs → more layers in the stackup.

---

### Lamination & material (short)

**Lamination choice**
- **FR4:** Economical; OK below ~2.5–3 GHz; digital can be affected by material.
- **Rogers (e.g. RO4350):** High‑speed / RF; Er stable with frequency.

**Material vs frequency**
- Signal velocity depends on **Er**. FR4: Er drops with frequency (e.g. ~4.7 → ~4 above 5 GHz). Rogers: Er ≈ 3.5 from 0–15 GHz. **Choose material for your frequency range.**

**Propagation velocity**
- \(v = c/\sqrt{E_r}\); slower than light in vacuum. If Er changes with frequency → different frequency components travel at different speeds → **distortion**. Loss also rises with f → more distortion. Use stable‑Er material for high‑speed.

---

### Stackup planning & material selection (short)

**Why stackup matters (4.1.7)**  
Good stackup reduces EMI, crosstalk, and improves SI; bad stackup increases EMI, crosstalk, and susceptibility. Planes: control crosstalk, provide reference voltages, distribute power. **Rules:** signal layer next to a plane; signal tightly coupled to adjacent plane (**&lt;10 mil**). Return path: high‑frequency current follows path of least inductance—use ground/power plane for it.

**Material parameters (4.2)**  
Key for RF/microwave: **Dk**, **Df**, CTE, TCDk, thermal conductivity. FR4 is common but not suitable for microwave; PTFE and Rogers are typical for high‑frequency.

**Material categories by speed/loss (4.2.1)**  
- **Normal speed, normal loss (e.g. FR-4, Isola 370HR):** Dk vs f not flat, higher loss; limited to a few GHz.  
- **Medium speed, medium loss (e.g. Nelco N7000-2 HT):** Flatter Dk vs f, ~half the loss; suitable to ~10 GHz.  
- **High speed, low loss (e.g. Isola I-Speed):** Flatter Dk, low loss; less electrical noise.  
- **Very high speed, very low loss / RF‑microwave (e.g. Isola Tachyon 100G):** Flattest Dk, least loss; to ~20 GHz.
